## 1. Basic (20 questions)

1) What is the total number of transactions in the dataset?  
Relevance: Shows overall data volume and that you can do simple counts (sanity check and data profiling).

2) How many transactions are labeled as fraud vs non‑fraud?  
Relevance: Gives fraud rate and class imbalance, which is critical context for any fraud or ML work.

3) What is the overall fraud rate (percentage of transactions that are fraud)?  
Relevance: Basic KPI every fraud analyst should know for a portfolio.

4) What is the total transaction amount and total fraud amount?  
Relevance: Connects transaction volume to monetary risk and fraud loss.

5) What is the average transaction amount for fraud vs non‑fraud?  
Relevance: Helps see whether fraud tends to hit higher or lower amounts.

6) What is the minimum, maximum, and average transaction amount overall?  
Relevance: Basic descriptive statistics to spot outliers or data issues.

7) How many distinct credit cards (cc_num) exist in the dataset?  
Relevance: Shows portfolio size and helps in later per‑card analysis.

8) How many distinct merchants are in the dataset?  
Relevance: Indicates merchant universe and potential for merchant‑level risk metrics.

9) How many transactions per year or per month?  
Relevance: Basic time‑distribution sanity check and seasonality starting point.

10) What is the count of transactions by transaction category (e.g., groceries, gas, etc.)?  
Relevance: Understands spending mix; later useful for category‑specific fraud patterns.

11) What is the fraud count and fraud rate by category?  
Relevance: Identifies which categories are fraud‑heavy and may need stricter controls.

12) What is the average transaction amount by category?  
Relevance: Provides typical ticket sizes and informs threshold rules.

13) What is the number of transactions by state?  
Relevance: Geographic distribution of your portfolio and activity.

14) What is the fraud count and fraud rate by state?  
Relevance: Quickly highlights high‑risk geographies.

15) What is the number of transactions by hour of day?  
Relevance: Time‑of‑day usage pattern; fraud often spikes at off‑hours.

16) What is the fraud count and fraud rate by hour of day?  
Relevance: Shows if fraud favors certain hours (e.g., night vs day).

17) For each merchant, how many transactions and how many frauds occurred?  
Relevance: Builds base for merchant risk ranking.

18) For each card, how many transactions and how many frauds occurred?  
Relevance: Lets you see “problem cards” and is the basis for per‑card risk features.

19) What is the distribution of transactions by customer gender?  
Relevance: Simple demographic slice; mostly for understanding data, not for rules.

20) What is the fraud rate by customer gender?  
Relevance: Demonstrates demographic slicing, but also highlights fairness / bias thinking.

## 2. Intermediate (20 questions)

21) For each card, what is the first date and last date of activity (transaction window)?  
Relevance: Shows card “lifetime” and signals dormant vs active cards.

22) For each card, what is the total transaction count, total amount, and fraud rate?  
Relevance: Key per‑card KPIs; you’ll reuse this as a feature table in ML.

23) For each merchant, what is the total transaction count, fraud count, fraud rate, and total fraud amount?  
Relevance: Core merchant risk metrics used in monitoring.

24) Among merchants with at least N transactions (e.g., 500), which top 10 have the highest fraud rate?  
Relevance: Avoids noise from tiny merchants and surfaces meaningful high‑risk merchants.

25) Among merchants with at least N transactions, which top 10 have the highest total fraud amount?  
Relevance: Focuses on loss exposure rather than rate alone.

26) What are the top 10 cards by total fraud amount?  
Relevance: Shows worst‑offending cards and supports case‑prioritization thinking.

27) For each hour of day, what is the average transaction amount for fraud vs non‑fraud?  
Relevance: Combines time and amount to see behavioral differences in fraud.

28) For each state, what is the total transaction amount and total fraud amount?  
Relevance: Geographic loss and volume view; often used for region‑level risk reporting.

29) For each category, what is the average fraud transaction amount vs average non‑fraud transaction amount?  
Relevance: Helps design category‑specific rules or dynamic thresholds.

30) For each card, what is the maximum number of transactions in a single day?  
Relevance: Captures bursty behavior; a common simple fraud rule input.

31) For each card, how many different merchants has it used?  
Relevance: Card “spread” across merchants can indicate potential compromise patterns.

32) For each merchant, how many distinct cards have transacted there?  
Relevance: Merchant popularity and potential exposure; useful for network‑style analysis.

33) For each card, what is the average time between its transactions?  
Relevance: Normal cadence vs spikes; basis for velocity rules.

34) For each city, what is the fraud rate and total fraud amount?  
Relevance: Finer‑grain geo risk than state, useful for more targeted insights.

35) For each month, what is the fraud rate trend over time?  
Relevance: Shows whether fraud is increasing, decreasing, or stable over the period.

36) What is the fraud rate for transactions above certain amount thresholds (e.g., >100, >500, >1,000)?  
Relevance: Classic cut analysis to understand risk by ticket size.

37) For each card, what proportion of its total amount is spent at its top 3 merchants?  
Relevance: Measures concentration; anomalously concentrated patterns can be suspicious.

38) For each merchant, what proportion of its transactions are from its top 5 cards?  
Relevance: Detects merchants heavily dependent on a few cards, which can be risky.

39) For each card, how many days had more than X transactions (e.g., >10)?  
Relevance: Identifies repeated high‑velocity days; signals potential ongoing abuse.

40) For each card, what is the fraud rate before and after a given date (e.g., midpoint of dataset)?  
Relevance: Simple “before/after” analysis, useful to illustrate cohort or period changes.

## 3. Advanced (20 questions)

41) For each card, list the first fraudulent transaction date and the number of days it took from first ever transaction to first fraud.  
Relevance: Measures “time‑to‑compromise,” a very realistic fraud metric.

42) For each card that had at least one fraud, how many legitimate transactions occurred in the 7 days before its first fraud?  
Relevance: Shows whether fraud is preceded by reconnaissance or normal spending.

43) For each card, list the three transactions immediately preceding its first fraud (by time).  
Relevance: Creates context around fraud events; classic windowed investigation query.

44) For each card, calculate rolling 24‑hour transaction count and total amount for every transaction (time‑window via window functions).  
Relevance: Time‑based velocity features used in near‑real‑time alerting.

45) For each card, calculate rolling 7‑day total transaction amount and compare it to the card’s overall average weekly amount.  
Relevance: Detects sudden spikes vs baseline behavior.

46) For each merchant, calculate monthly fraud rate, then find merchants whose fraud rate increased by more than X percentage points month‑over‑month.  
Relevance: Flags merchants whose risk profile is worsening, which is operationally actionable.

47) For each card, compute the number of distinct states in which it transacted, and the maximum distance between any two transaction locations (approximate via lat/long).  
Relevance: Captures geographic spread; large sudden jumps suggest possible compromise.

48) For each fraud transaction, find the previous transaction of the same card and compute time difference and distance between locations.  
Relevance: Classic “impossible travel” style query for card‑not‑present / geo‑based rules.

49) For each category, compute fraud rate stratified by time‑of‑day bucket (e.g., night vs day).  
Relevance: Combines category and temporal dimensions for more nuanced rules.

50) For each merchant, compute the share of fraud amount coming from its top 10 cards.  
Relevance: Shows whether merchant fraud is concentrated in a small set of cards.

51) For each card, compute the Gini or simple concentration metric of spend across merchants (e.g., Herfindahl index).  
Relevance: More advanced feature for “behavioral profile” used in ML and rules.

52) For each card, identify the day with its highest fraud amount and report that day’s total transaction amount, total fraud amount, and number of distinct merchants.  
Relevance: Focuses on “peak fraud days” and whether they look like concentrated attacks.

53) Identify merchants whose fraud rate is significantly above the overall portfolio fraud rate (e.g., > 2 standard deviations above mean fraud rate across merchants).  
Relevance: Adds a mild statistical flavor to merchant risk ranking.

54) Build a per‑card “home state” as the state where the card has most transactions, then compute fraud rate for in‑home vs out‑of‑home‑state transactions.  
Relevance: Very realistic rule: out‑of‑home or out‑of‑region transactions often riskier.

55) For each card, compute the share of its total amount that occurred at night (e.g., 22:00–06:00), then compare this distribution for fraud vs non‑fraud cards.  
Relevance: Behavioral feature; helps show if fraud cards behave differently in time usage.

56) For each card, count the number of different categories used in the last 30 days of the dataset vs previous 30 days.  
Relevance: Behavior drift detection at category level.

57) For each merchant, compute the ratio of fraud count to unique cards, and unique cards to total transactions.  
Relevance: Separates “many small frauds across many cards” vs “few cards heavily hit.”

58) For each card, detect days where the number of distinct merchants exceeds a dynamic threshold (e.g., card’s average + 3 standard deviations) and list those days.  
Relevance: Outlier detection on merchant spread per day.

59) For each card, classify each transaction as “local” or “non‑local” based on distance from its most frequent city, then compute fraud rate by this local/non‑local flag.  
Relevance: Adds behavioral + geo logic similar to real‑world scoring models.

60) Rank cards by a composite risk score built from normalized features: fraud rate, max daily amount, % spend at night, and number of distinct states, computed in SQL.  
Relevance: Mimics a simplified risk score using only SQL aggregation and normalization.

## 4. Expert (20 questions)

61) Build a per‑card feature table suitable for ML in SQL: one row per card with dozens of aggregated features (e.g., total_txn, total_amt, fraud_rate, avg_amt, max_daily_amt, #merchants, #states, %night_spend, time_to_first_fraud, etc.).  
Relevance: Shows you can engineer production‑ready features for ML directly in SQL.

62) Build a per‑merchant feature table suitable for merchant risk modeling in SQL, with metrics including monthly fraud trends, concentration of cards, and geographic spread of their customers.  
Relevance: Merchant underwriting / monitoring style features, showing domain depth.

63) Using SQL only, simulate a simple rule‑based fraud detection system (e.g., transactions flagged as suspicious if: amount > X, night transaction, at high‑risk merchant, etc.) and compute precision/recall against `is_fraud`.  
Relevance: Bridges from descriptive analytics to evaluation of detection rules.

64) Implement a time‑ordered split in SQL (e.g., 2019 as “train” and 2020 as “test”) and compute how a given rule’s performance changes between train and test.  
Relevance: Shows understanding of concept drift and model/rule stability over time.

65) Using window functions, measure the “burstiness” of fraud by computing, for each fraud transaction, how many other frauds occur on the same card within ±1 day, ±3 days, ±7 days.  
Relevance: Captures fraud clustering; useful for case‑linking or network analysis.

66) Identify potential collusive merchant–card pairs by finding merchants where a small subset of cards contributes a large share of both volume and fraud, and the cards also share similar high‑risk patterns elsewhere.  
Relevance: Introduces latent network / collusion reasoning with pure SQL.

67) Tag each transaction with a basic behavioral segment of the card (e.g., “low volume”, “medium volume”, “high volume”) based on distribution of per‑card monthly volume, using quantiles computed in SQL.  
Relevance: Shows segmentation logic built directly in SQL, common in risk analytics.

68) Build a SQL query that approximates an “account takeover” pattern: cards where the last 5 transactions in the dataset all occurred in states that were never seen earlier for that card.  
Relevance: Very realistic fraud scenario, uses temporal and distinct‑value logic.

69) For each card, compute a “geo velocity” feature: distance per hour between consecutive transactions, then flag transactions above a high threshold as “impossible travel” candidates.  
Relevance: Classic advanced fraud rule implemented with window functions and haversine‑style distance in SQL.

70) For each merchant, compute an “anomaly score” based on how different its fraud rate and ticket size distribution are from the average within its category (e.g., z‑scores).  
Relevance: Shows category‑peer comparison and outlier scoring for merchants.

71) Create a SQL‑only pipeline (using CTEs) that: filters to a time window, builds per‑card features, applies rule thresholds, and outputs a final scored transaction table for that window.  
Relevance: Demonstrates end‑to‑end, pipeline‑style thinking just in SQL.

72) Using only SQL, compute a simple calibration table: for a rule that assigns a discrete risk score (1–5), compute for each score the empirical fraud rate.  
Relevance: Shows understanding of risk score calibration and monitoring.

73) Create a SQL query that simulates a sliding training window: for each month M, use data from months M‑3 to M‑1 to compute per‑card stats, and then evaluate a rule on month M.  
Relevance: Captures production‑style rolling window model/rule updates.

74) Build card‑to‑merchant bipartite counts and then derive, in SQL, for each pair of merchants, how many cards they share, flagging highly connected merchants.  
Relevance: Introduces network/graph intuition, useful for organized fraud rings.

75) For each card, compute a “category novelty” score over time: share of amount spent in categories the card has not used in the prior 60 days, per transaction.  
Relevance: Dynamic behavioral drift feature that resembles advanced ML features.

76) Implement a SQL query that, for each fraud transaction, finds a small set of “similar” non‑fraud transactions (same merchant, similar amount, close in time) to illustrate how hard it can be to distinguish fraud.  
Relevance: Demonstrates appreciation of fraud detection difficulty and sampling logic.

77) Derive age at transaction from `dob` and `trans_date_trans_time`, then compute fraud rate by age band and category combined.  
Relevance: Combines derived demographics with behavior, useful for nuanced risk insights.

78) Build a per‑day portfolio summary that includes not just simple aggregates but also distribution metrics (e.g., percentiles of amount, % at night, # cards with >10 txns) and then study how these daily features differ between high‑fraud days and low‑fraud days.  
Relevance: Portfolio‑level monitoring, helpful for real‑time dashboards and alerts.

79) In SQL, implement a basic “outlier detection” for transaction amounts within each category using IQR or z‑score logic and compute how many of those outliers are fraud vs non‑fraud.  
Relevance: Connects statistical anomaly detection with fraud labels.

80) Design a SQL query that outputs, for each card, a compact text (or JSON) profile summarizing its key behavioral features and risk indicators (e.g., `"card_profile": {"home_state":"CA","night_spend_pct":0.3,...}` using JSON functions).  
Relevance: Shows use of advanced PostgreSQL features (JSON) and how SQL can feed downstream APIs or services.
